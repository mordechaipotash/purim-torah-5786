## Mesibas Chasal, Pesach 5733

### The Blessing of Your Festival

> *"And confer upon us Hashem . . . the blessing of Your festival . . . in accordance with Your wish and Your promise to bless us"* — from the Yom Tov Amidah

I know of an individual who, when inscribing blessings, would preface them with the words *"Yehi rotzon . . ."* (may it be Hashem's will) — a practice to which certain great men objected.

Their reason: **Yehi rotzon is an expression of prayer, distinct from a blessing.**

- **Prayer** is offered with the object of bringing about *new* conditions — arousing Divine will where none previously existed
- **Blessing** however, is conferred where the will and conditions *already exist* — its function is to facilitate the fruition of the will by removing any impediments to its realization

The sole exception is *Bircas Cohanim*, where the posuk promises: *"And they shall confer My Name upon bnei Yisroel and I will bless them"* (Bamidbar 6:27). Conferring Hashem's Name gives rise to a wish that did not exist beforehand, for His Name is limitless and its mention can arouse a new desire to bestow benefit.

**The word *brochoh* (blessing) is related to the word *bereichoh* (a pond or pool).** Just as a pond is nourished by a preceding source, so blessing flows from an already existing wish to bestow benefit. This is why we ask Hashem to *"confer upon us the blessing of Your festival . . . in accordance with Your rotzon and Your promise to bless us."*

### The Season of Our Freedom

Pesach is called "the season of our freedom." The type of freedom we celebrate is exemplified by an incident involving **Rav Yisroel Salanter zt"l**:

Rav Yisroel rebuked a person who was unable to break a habit, telling him he was transgressing:

> *"For bnei Yisroel are servants of Mine; they are My servants"* (Vayikra 25:55)

Chazal infer: **"My servants — and not servants to servants"** (Bava Metzia 10), which Rav Yisroel took to mean **"not slaves of habit."**

A more refined way of sidestepping the obligation to serve Hashem Alone is for a person to say that *in a particular situation or in certain conditions* he is unable to serve Hashem. He makes his service subject to outside circumstances, giving them mastery over himself.

> *"When a person is brought in for judgment he is asked . . . 'Did you await Hashem's salvation?'"* — Shabbos 31

**Rabbenu Yonah** (*Shaarei Teshuvah, Shaar* IV:8) writes that a penitent *"should yearn and wait for Yom Kippur in order to find favor with Hashem."* This is how he awaits Hashem's salvation. His defense for having sinned is:

> *"We want to fulfill Your will; what prevents us is the leaven in the dough [the yetzer hora] and the yoke of foreign rule"* — Brochos 17

If he isn't waiting for Yom Kippur and the acceptance of his repentance, he cannot invoke this claim in his favor.

During Pesach — "the season of our freedom" — we are free of all interfering factors. **Now, as the Yom Tov departs, only the yearning for this freedom will remain with us** — the longing to be free from all other yokes so that we can serve Hashem Alone. This is how we await Hashem's salvation.

### Our Own Connection

> *"I am Hashem . . . Who brought you out of the land of Egypt"* — Shemos 20:2

The Rishonim discuss whether this statement is counted as one of the 613 mitzvos, but all agree it is the foundation and cornerstone of faith.

**Why don't we learn *Shaar Hayichud* in *Chovos Halevovos* today?** There are chapters of Rav Saadiah Gaon's *Sefer Ho'emunos* and the Rambam's *Moreh Nevuchim* on related topics that we also refrain from studying. Bnei Torah have asked to be told why.

**There are two reasons, which at first glance seem contradictory:**

1. **Our generation's level is low**
2. **It has special merit**

In fact, these are two sides of the same coin.

#### The Consequence of Our Low Level

A parable: A person hears a melody that captures his heart so deeply that it feels almost tangible — were it possible to appreciate it using his sense of sight, he would yearn to experience it that way as well.

The faith of earlier generations was so real to them that they longed to experience it in as many ways as possible. Their occupation with philosophical aspects of faith indicates their virtue; **our lack of such interest is a sign of the paucity of faith in our times**.

Moreover, nowadays confusion predominates — were we to get involved in investigating the nature of faith, we would conclude that there is room for discussion and that certain things require proof — **which is absolutely not the case**.

#### Our Special Greatness

> *"For with those who are here . . . And with those who are not here"* — Devorim 29:14

Since the souls of all future Jews were present when the Torah was given, they must also have been present when bnei Yisroel left Egypt, for Hashem said at Har Sinai: *"I am Hashem . . . Who brought you out of Egypt."*

**All generations are thus on an equal footing with regard to the fundamentals of belief.** *(See Pachad Yitzchok, Pesach 33:2.)*

> *"And He will return the hearts of fathers to sons"* — Malachi 3:24

Rashi explains: the fathers' hearts will be brought back to Hashem *through their sons*. This was the last prophecy written down for Klal Yisroel — **the novelty of the reversal of the usual chain of transmission, from sons to fathers**.

In the usual framework of *"you shall teach them to your sons"* (Devorim 6:7), the failure of a single generation to transmit would mean the loss of every subsequent generation. However, all generations were present at Har Sinai and have an independent bond — so transmission from son to father can take place.

**This is happening nowadays wherever there are Jews.** This is why our generation needs no proofs of faith. Our power derives mainly from the fact that *we too* were present at Har Sinai, for today we see the prophecy *"He will return the hearts of fathers to sons"* being fulfilled before our very eyes.

Hashem's words — *"Who brought you out of Egypt"* — were said to us. *"You have seen . . ."* (Shemos 19:4) — this shows our greatness.

A ben Torah with depth of insight will see that there is no contradiction between these two reasons; they represent two aspects of one situation.

### Valuing the Lessons of the Yom Tov

> *"Eisov scorned the birthright"* — Bereishis 25:34

Some mussar scholars have pointed out that since this is not part of the actual sale, it seems out of place. The truth is otherwise:

**High levels of holiness are extremely sensitive.** Attaining them and holding on to them requires acknowledgment of how precious they are. If a person upon whom they have been conferred scorns them, they leave him forthwith. Eisov's belittling the birthright lost it to him.

*(Ed. note: The Ketzos Hachoshen (278:13) suggests that by saying "Why do I need the birthright?" Eisov effectively renounced it!)*

> *"The attribute of good is more extensive than that of retribution"* — Sotah 11

**When a person respects and values these high levels, they attach themselves to him.**

Brazenness shown to a Torah scholar when he is not present does not have the same significance as when shown to his face. Conversely, **honoring a scholar even when he is not around shows greater respect**.

**Valuing the freedom of serving Hashem at a Mesibas Chasal — when we are no longer in the presence of the Yom Tov and its spiritual treasures — causes those treasures to attach themselves to us.**

## Mesibas Chasal, Pesach 5734

### A Parting that Leaves a Lasting Impression

> *"A person should rise for prayer neither in depressed nor in lazy spirits . . . rather, as experiencing the joy of a mitzvah. Similarly, a person should not part from his friend with conversation, mirth, levity, or with idle chatter, rather, with a matter of halochoh . . ."* — Brochos 31

**A person's soul overflows with myriad impressions.** Everything that there is in the world has its corresponding element within the human soul. The secret of life lies in the combination of all the minor etchings on the soul, rather than with the major events that happen to a person in his lifetime.

**Rav Yisroel** also held that all the tiny impressions combine to have an effect. He cites the example of Rabbi Akiva (*Avos DeRabbi Nosson, perek* 6:2):

> *"What were Rabbi Akiva's origins? . . . He was once standing by a wellspring and asked, 'Who hollowed out this stone?' . . . They said to him, 'Akiva, don't you know the posuk, "Water wore away stones"?' Rabbi Akiva immediately reasoned with himself . . . Then and there he went back to learn Torah."*

Even if a mitzvah has already been done, one should pray soon after — for the impression it has made and the spiritual uplift from the joy of having performed it will make his prayers different. Similarly, parting from a friend with a matter of halochoh makes it a different kind of parting.

**The Mesibas Chasal is *"paved within with love"* (Shir Hashirim 3:10)** — it adjoins the Yom Tov and is suffused with festive joy and the warmth of friendship. There is also the wish to preserve the impression of the Yom Tov by taking leave from it at the Mesibas Chasal, **so that the coming year in its entirety will carry an impression of the Yom Tov**.

### Days of Greatness

**In response to the question:** Why don't we deliver talks on ethics and personal service of Hashem all year round, whereas on the festivals we speak about them so much?

The answer is based upon Chazal:

> *"What is the meaning of 'For he who scorned the day of smallness'? (Zecharya 4:10) What causes the future reward of tzaddikim to be incomplete? Their small-mindedness in not believing in Hashem"* — Sotah 48

Rashi explains: "Smallness — paucity of faith."

This cannot mean that their faith was weak, because the posuk doesn't say a word about faith — it just speaks about a "day of smallness."

**This type of smallness determines the way in which one views the world and all it contains.** A person who looks at the world with a small, petty outlook is by definition a person of little faith — he sees just the world, not the world's Creator.

On the other hand, **when a person looks at the world and sees greatness and importance, it can only be because he sees that the world comes from Hashem.** The rule is that anything done in a spirit of greatness and importance is itself a greater thing — and makes a greater impression.

When we speak about ethics and character traits — matters that pierce the heavens — **it must be done in an atmosphere of the most sublime greatness and importance possible**. During routine weeks, that dimension is missing. On festivals, when *"You have exalted us"* — bnei Torah in particular feel uplifted and look at the world with greatness and importance.

> Our master said that that partial fulfillment is worth all the treasures in the world!

### The Pious Shall Sing

> *"Pious men will rejoice in His glory; they will sing from their place of repose"* — Tehillim 149:5

**Chassidus** means doing more than one is required to do (*Menochos* 41: *"Chassidim are different because they are stringent with themselves."* Rashi: *"The chossid goes beyond the demands of the din."*).

People wrongly equate chassidus with frumkeit and a lack of thoughtful reckoning. *(See Mesillas Yeshorim, perek 18.)* The Gemara shows us this view is superficial:

#### Shimon HaTzaddik and the Nozir from the South

> *"Shimon HaTzaddik said, 'I never ate from the oshom offering of a nozir who had become impure, except for once. A man once came, a nozir from the South. I saw that he had beautiful eyes and a handsome appearance and his locks of hair were in curls.*
>
> *I asked him, 'My son, why have you seen fit to spoil this beautiful hair of yours?'*
>
> *He said, 'I was working as my father's shepherd. I went to fill up with water from the well and looked at my reflection and my yetzer hora pounced on me. I told him: Evil one! Why are you taking pride in a world that does not belong to you, in one who is ultimately going to be rot and worms? I swear to shave you off for the sake of Heaven!'*
>
> *I immediately arose and kissed him on the head."* — Nedorim 9

**The Ran** explains that halachically, the nozir's regret cannot change his status automatically (unless a chochom annuls it), so there was no question of the offering's validity. **But Shimon HaTzaddik, viewing through the lens of chassidus, felt the offering's sanctity appeared flawed** — even though it was 100% valid according to halochoh.

**Chassidus represents an entirely different way of viewing things** — springing from a higher, more refined level of living that relates to every realm of life.

#### Rav Safra's Truth

> *"And he speaks the truth in his heart"* — Tehillim 15:2

Rav Safra had an article to sell. A man came while he was reading Krias Shema and offered a price. He didn't answer because he was reading. The man raised his offer. After finishing, Rav Safra said: *"Take it for the first price — because I had in mind to sell it to you for that."*

The **Maharsha** explains that Rav Safra fulfilled a mental commitment to another person — which halochoh only requires when dealing with *hekdesh* (the Temple treasury), which acquires ownership based on thought alone: *"each person who donates in his heart"* (Shemos 35:5).

**Rav Safra was not merely stringent in truthfulness — his entire life was on a more sublime and exalted level, both in thought and in deed.** What he thought in his heart took on tangibility; he treated the ordinary and mundane in the way prescribed for the holy.

#### Rabbenu Yonah's Definition

> *"A boor cannot fear sin and an am ho'oretz cannot be pious"* — Avos 2:5

Rabbenu Yonah explains: An am ho'oretz can even be a tzaddik — but **only someone great in Torah can attain chassidus**. This is a trait that requires a pure heart and a refined soul, and the am ho'oretz lacks the wisdom needed to shift from the middle path to the outer extreme.

**To be a chossid one must be great in Torah because the chossid inhabits another world, where the greatness and importance of Torah reign supreme. His world is a different world entirely.**

> *"For a mitzvah is a lamp and Torah is light"* — Mishlei 6:23

Chazal comment: *"Just as a lamp only offers temporary protection, so does a mitzvah. Light gives complete protection and so does Torah"* (Sotah 21).

**A mitzvah done on the level of chassidus, however, also gives everlasting protection** — because its performance arises from Torah's greatness and it contains the light of Torah as well.

Thus: *"Chassidim will rejoice — because of Torah's greatness and importance. They will sing from their place of repose — even when they are not occupied with it, because its effect is everlasting."*

### A New Song

**Rabbenu Yonah** (*Shaarei Teshuvah, Shaar* III:148) writes that one of the ways of sanctifying Hashem's Name is to proclaim **in every expression, in every glance and in every act and activity** that man's primary purpose is to serve and fear Hashem.

This requires that *each and every function* be involved because everything needs to fit its inner content. The central feature of Heaven's glory is *"the whole world is full of His glory"* (Yeshayahu 6:3). Everything undertaken for Hashem's honor must therefore accord with this and **not have any vacuum**.

> *"The ways of your youth will be as refreshing to you as dew"* — Tehillim 110:3

The medrash: *"Avrohom Ovinu was fearful and said, 'Perhaps I carry guilt for having worshipped idols for all those years?' HaKodosh Boruch Hu told him, 'Your youth will be to you as dew'"* (Bereishis Rabbah 39:8). This is because **"a new convert is like a newborn babe"** (Yevomos 22).

Upon leaving Egypt, Knesses Yisroel became converts *(see Rashi, Yevomos 46)*. The words *"your youth will be to you as dew"* thus apply to Knesses Yisroel in relation to the entire creation — for **it is in them that the world's renewal takes place, with the reversion to the form of Adam HaRishon**.

The song after crossing the sea contains a vision of the future — the renewal of man's form revealed in Knesses Yisroel. As befits its inner content, this song is termed ***Shiroh Chadoshoh*** — a new song.

### The Spoils of the Sea

> *"Bestow gifts on him . . . and remember that you were a servant in Egypt . . ."* — Devorim 15:14

The mitzvah to give gifts to a departing servant has its roots in the departure from Egypt.

**Our master said that the goal of the Mesibas Chasal is that everyone should take something away from the Yom Tov** that befits his own trials and circumstances. He should carry a sense of renewal into the coming year, drawing from a sense of greatness and importance!

> *"Moshe moved bnei Yisroel on from the Yam Suf"* — Shemos 15:22

Rashi explains that he forced them to travel on because they were gleaning the valuable decorations with which the Egyptians had adorned their army from the sea. **The spoils of the Yam Suf surpassed the spoils of Egypt.** It was necessary to compel them to move on against their will.

However, we don't find that he had to use sticks to force them. He had to compel them to leave the spoils of the sea behind in order to move on to **receive the Torah**.

> Right now, we don't want to leave the spoils of the sea — but like it or not, we must do so, returning to routine, sitting and learning with love and application, and going forward to receive the Torah. **In this way, "the spoils of the sea" will come to us!**

### The Moment of Parting

In 5737, our master ended the Mesibas Chasal with the closing words of the Amidah:

> *"He who makes peace in His heights, He should make peace for us and for all of Yisroel."*

Our request is in the third person — *"He* should make peace" — because this is the moment of parting.

He added that in a way, parting from the Yom Tov at the Mesibas Chasal is like a talmid parting from his master:

> **"A person who has never witnessed a talmid taking leave of his teacher — and the strengthening of their bond that is so strong — has never seen refinement (*eidelkeit*) and has never seen a leave-taking in his life."**
