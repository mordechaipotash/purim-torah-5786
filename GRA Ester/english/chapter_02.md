## Commentary of the Vilna Gaon (GRA) on Megillas Esther: Chapter 2

---

## The Plain Interpretation (Peshat)

**(1) That which she had done and that which was decreed against her** — In the Gemara (Megillah 12b): "Just as she had done, so it was decreed against her." **The explanation is:** It was difficult for them [the Sages] that it is written "which was decreed against her," which implies [a decree] from Heaven, even though it might not have been appropriate to do so to her; and what is the meaning of the expression "decreed"? Therefore, they explained that "that which she had done" refers to Israel—just as she [Vashti] had done to Israel [forcing Jewish girls to work on Shabbos], so it was decreed against her from Heaven.

**(2) Young virgins** — "Young women" and not old ones; "virgins" and not those who had already had relations. And it uses the plural language, meaning all the young women who were of beautiful appearance from the entire city.

**(3) And let the king appoint officers... and let them gather every young woman, etc.** — **The explanation is:** That the officers should gather from all the young women one girl from the best of them all, and afterward, even the best among them would be given into the hand of Hegai.
**And let their ointments be given** — **The explanation is:** As they said in Tractate Ta’anit (31a): "The beautiful among them would say, 'Young man, lift up your eyes and see what you choose for yourself, for a woman is only for beauty'; the well-born among them would say, 'Young man, lift up your eyes and look at the family'; the ugly among them would say, 'Take your purchase for the sake of Heaven, provided that you crown me with gold [jewelry].'" Now, this is a wonder: if one wants to take a wife for the sake of Heaven, he should take the well-born! However, the matter is that a purchase made for an ulterior motive is not good, and in both [the beautiful and the well-born] there is an ulterior motive. But with the ugly ones, [who say] "crown me with gold and we will become pleasant," it will be for the sake of Heaven and we will also be beautiful. It emerges that the more a woman adorns herself, the more beautiful she is. For this reason, it says "and let their ointments be given from the king's house"—they were all equal. For if they had all adorned themselves on their own, they would not have known which one was of more beautiful appearance, for this one might adorn herself more and this one less.

**(4) And let the maiden, etc.** — **The explanation is:** One maiden from all the maidens.
**Reign in the place of Vashti** — **The explanation is:** Memuchan had said "to her peer" (1:19), meaning one of noble lineage like her. But the king's attendants said "of beautiful appearance," for "what do I care about lineage? For whoever she may be, she will reign in the place of Vashti, and there is no practical difference regarding lineage." Similarly, they said "Vashti" and did not say "Queen Vashti."

**(5) A Jewish man (*Ish Yehudi*)** — In the Gemara they said (Megillah 12b): "He is called *Yehudi* [from Judah] and he is called *Yemini* [from Benjamin]." They answered: "He was crowned with his laws (*nimusin*)." Now, this Gemara is not [easily] understood and is very wondrous; are they not all called *Yehudim* (Jews), as is written in this Megillah many times?
At first glance, it would seem that their difficulty stems from the word *Ish* (man), which is a term for a prince or person of importance, as in "Are you not a man (*Ish*), and who is like you..." (I Samuel 26:15). But even this is not a difficulty, for one could say he was a prince.
It appears that their difficulty is from the word *Ish* as mentioned, and that it appears twice. For the reason they are called *Yehudim* is because the Land of Israel was divided into Judah and the Galilee, and since those in the Galilee were exiled in the days of Sennacherib, only the tribe of Judah remained. Even though some from Benjamin and other tribes remained—whom Jeremiah brought back—since they all went into exile and only a few remained, they too were included in the tribe of Judah.
This is what was difficult for them: "He is called *Yehudi*," implying he is now a prince over all of Judah, who are all of Israel called by the name *Yehudim*; and "he is called *Ish Yemini*," implying he was originally a prince when they were on their land, when each tribe was called by its own name. To this they answered that he was "crowned with laws," meaning with two crowns: he was originally a prince over the tribe of Benjamin, and afterward when they were exiled, he also became a prince over all the Jews, and his first title also remained.
**Was in Shushan the capital** — **The explanation is:** Because he was there first, and Ahasuerus came because of him, as mentioned above (1:2); therefore it is written "He was (*Hayah*)."
**The son of Jair, the son of Shimei, the son of Kish** — In the Gemara they said (Megillah 12b): "Son of Jair—who enlightened (*he'ir*) the eyes of Israel with his prayer; son of Shimei—for God heard (*shama*) his prayer; son of Kish—who knocked (*hikish*) on the gates of mercy."
Now, this Gemara is a very exalted wonder: why did they expound thus? Could they not expound similarly on any name, even a hundred times? It appears that because in man there are four senses: sight, hearing, smell, and speech. Three of them are for the study of Torah: sight is to see the Written Torah; hearing is to hear the Oral Torah; speech is to teach others. But smell is not needed for Torah at all, but rather for the aspect of prayer, as it says, "a fire-offering, a pleasing aroma (*rei'ach nicho'ach*) to Hashem" (Leviticus 1:9). Mordechai represents the aspect of smell, as it says "pure myrrh (*mordechai*)" (Exodus 30:23), which the Targum renders as *mari dachia* [pure master], which is the aspect of prayer. Therefore they said that all these aspects were in his prayer: "Jair"—that he enlightened the eyes of Israel, which is sight; "Shimei"—that He heard his prayer, which is hearing; "Kish"—that he knocked, which is speech. These four senses correspond to the four letters of the Name [Y-H-V-H]. This is why it says first "A Jewish man (*Ish Yehudi*)" and then "A Benjamite man (*Ish Yemini*)," as mentioned, for they are in the general and the specific.

**(6) Who had been exiled from Jerusalem, etc.** — It says "exiled" here three times: "who had been exiled from Jerusalem," "who had been exiled [with the exile]," "whom [Nebuchadnezzar] had exiled," to make known his love for the Land of Israel—that each time he returned to Jerusalem and was exiled three times. This is what they meant (Megillah 13a) that "he went into exile on his own," meaning by his own will he returned to the Land of Israel, and because of that, he was exiled a second time, and likewise a third time. And it uses the language of exile four times here to indicate that all four exiles will remember his redemption, as it is said, "and their memory shall not perish" (9:28).
**With Jeconiah king of Judah** — To indicate his greatness, that he was a Benjamite yet he was the head among them, as it says "A Jewish man (*Ish Yehudi*)."

**(7) Hadassah, she is Esther** — **The explanation is:** Her name was "Hadassah," and the nations called her "Esther" after the name of the star *Istahar* [Venus], as stated in the Gemara (13a). And it says here that he was raising Hadassah, who is called Esther. In the Gemara, they [infer] from the fact that it mentions Hadassah at all, since she is called Esther throughout the Megillah, and they said her name was Esther because of her righteousness (*tzidkus*), to say that they understood immediately when she was born that she would be righteous.
**His uncle's daughter, etc.** — It states here the reason that led to him raising her, and mentions three things: 1) She was his uncle's daughter. 2) She had no father or mother—meaning she never had a father or mother [even for a moment]. 3) She was of beautiful form and beautiful appearance—as will be explained later with Heaven's help.
Regarding Esther, it says "beautiful form (*yefas to'ar*)," but when the king's servants spoke, they only said "good appearance (*tovas mareh*)." This is as it says, "I am black but comely" (Song of Songs 1:5). They said (Shir HaShirim Rabbah 1:35): "I am black in my deeds, but comely in the deeds of my ancestors." Now, this is not understood at all. It appears there are two types of beauty: one who is beautiful in appearance (*mareh*), and one who is dark in appearance but whose limbs are beautiful—meaning they are in proper proportion, neither too long nor too short, such as the nose and other limbs. This is what is meant by "I am black"—even though I am black and have no good deeds, nevertheless I am beautiful in the "cut of the limbs," which are the commandments themselves that I perform like the righteous, even though I do not know their secret and intention.
So too here: "beautiful form (*yefas to'ar*)" refers to the cut of the limbs, which correspond to the commandments; "beautiful appearance (*yefas mareh*)" refers to good character traits and the intention of the heart. Therefore Esther, who was a Jewess, possessed "beautiful form," the cut of the limbs corresponding to the commandments, and "beautiful appearance," which is good traits and a good heart. But the gentiles possess only "good appearance" [natural traits]. Therefore they [the servants] only said "good appearance." Mordechai, who saw in Esther the beautiful cut of limbs and also good appearance, understood she would be righteous in the performance of commandments and also a possessor of good traits, as it says "The recognition of their faces witnesses against them" (Isaiah 3:9); therefore he took her to himself as a daughter.
**And at the death of her father and mother, etc.** — This is the timing; meaning, from when did he take her? At the death of her father and mother.

**(8) And it came to pass, when the king's word and his decree were heard** — **The explanation is:** At first, Esther was hiding herself. But afterward, when they saw they had not brought Esther, they went back and issued a decree (*das*) that anyone who has a young woman and does not bring her shall be put to death, as stated in the Midrash (Targum Sheni). Then she was revealed and they took her against her will. This is "the king's word"—which was the first command; and "his decree"—which was the law he established that if they do not bring her, they will be put to death.
**And when [many maidens] were gathered, etc.** — **The explanation is:** And even this was only so that they would all be gathered.
**Esther was taken** — Against her will, to show the greatness of her compulsion, that it was not by her desire.

**(9) And the maiden pleased [him]... and she obtained kindness** — For a woman needs two virtues: 1) that she be of good appearance, and 2) that she find favor and kindness before him because she is pious (*chasidah*). This is what they said (Ketubot 17a): "A beautiful and graceful (*chasudah*) bride," as mentioned.
**And he hastened, etc.** — **The explanation is:** Everything that needed to be given to her, he gave with speed, for he understood she would be queen.
**And the seven maidens, etc.** — **The explanation is:** Those maidens given from the king's house to a queen, he gave to her immediately, for he understood she would certainly be queen.
**To the best [part] of the house of the women** — **The explanation is:** He changed [her location] and gave her and her maidens a house better than all the other women, for they were the queen's maidens.

**(10) Esther did not tell, etc.** — **The explanation is:** For Mordechai had commanded her not to tell her people or her kindred. This was because she had been hiding herself from the king, and even after she was discovered, she did not go until they took her against her will. Mordechai was afraid they would kill her, her family, and her people, for they would say they do not wish to intermarry even with the king.
Now the verse shows us the greatness of her righteousness: Mordechai only commanded her out of fear that they might kill her, yet she saw that they were treating her with great honor and not mentioning what had happened; nevertheless, "Esther did not tell." This is because "Mordechai had commanded her not to tell," and she kept Mordechai's command even though she knew the reason no longer applied.

**(11) To know the welfare of Esther and what would be done with her** — For Mordechai was distressed over two things: 1) He knew Esther was a great righteous woman, and the "good" of the wicked is evil to her, and she was greatly distressed by this; he was afraid she might fall ill from the greatness of the distress. 2) He was also distressed and afraid they might kill her because she had hidden herself and did not want to go, as mentioned. This is the meaning of "to know the welfare of Esther"—meaning if she is not ill; "and what would be done with her"—if they had not killed her.

## The Allegorical Interpretation (Remez)

**(5) A Benjamite man (*Ish Yemini*)** — [The soul] is called "from the right," as it says "And you, go to the end... to the portion of the right" (Daniel 12:13), and as it says "A wise man's heart is at his right" (Ecclesiastes 10:2).

**(7) And he was raising Hadassah** — **The explanation is:** He raised the soul, not the body, so that it would not follow its lusts. "Hadassah" is the body in this world, and why is it called Hadassah? On account of the myrtle (*hadas*), as it says "and he stands among the myrtles" (Zechariah 1:8).
**She is Esther** — For they [the nations] call her by the name of the moon (*Istahar*), for the righteous are called by the name of the moon, as is known.
**For she had no father or mother** — As it says, "Who said of his father and mother, 'I have not seen them'" (Deuteronomy 33:9) [referring to the soul's detachment from worldly origins].
**And the maiden was of beautiful form, etc.** — Desirable above [to God] and pleasant below [to man].
**And at the death of her father and mother** — After she removed from herself all the traits of this world, then Mordechai took her to himself as a daughter.

**(12) And when the turn of every maiden came, etc.** — According to the plain meaning, there are superfluous words here that are not necessary at all. It appears they hinted to us in this regarding the soul, which is called a "maiden."
**According to the law of the women** — **The explanation is:** The law of a woman is to give her twelve months for ornaments (Ketubot 57a). This means that every Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur, a person returns in repentance and cries out "I have sinned, forgive me," and the Holy One, Blessed be He, in His great kindness and mercy, passes over his sins and iniquities.
**The explanation is:** The iniquity itself is not erased but remains suspended [to see] if he will return in complete repentance, as I will write later; [if so] He will forgive, but if not, He will not forgive. Such is the judgment of the Holy One, Blessed be He: even though for a sin he commits later he might not be so deserving of that punishment, yet because of past sins, He punishes him. This is what is said: "If your children sinned against Him, He delivered them into the hand of their transgression" (Job 8:4). **The explanation is:** Even though they only committed a sin that was unintentional and it was not fitting to deliver them for that, He delivered them into the hand of the transgressions they already had, and now, because of the [new] sin, they were reckoned.
This is what we say [in liturgy]: "Because of our sins and the iniquities of our fathers, the Temple was destroyed." **The explanation is:** Because of the iniquities of our fathers the Temple was destroyed, and now because of our sins—for which we might not be liable for such a result—nevertheless it is not rebuilt because they reckon our sins along with the iniquities of our fathers. This is why they wait for her a period of twelve months.
**For so were the days of their ointments fulfilled** — **The explanation is:** The days in which they would cleanse (*yamriku*) themselves from transgressions.
**Six months with oil of myrrh** — **The explanation is:** For the Holy One, Blessed be He, created everything in man for a purpose, except for the hair, which is superfluous; it corresponds to the permitted things that a person does not need to have. This is hinted at in the portion of "When you go out to war," as stated in the Zohar: "and she shall shave her head and do her nails" (Deuteronomy 21:12), which are the superfluities.
This is why it says "six months with oil of myrrh." They said in the Gemara (13a): "What is oil of myrrh? *Stachis*, which removes hair." This means he should cast away superfluous desires for all six months.
And for six months he should habituate himself in the commandments, which are positive and negative. This is "with perfumes," which are the positive commandments, and "with the ointments of women," which are the negative commandments—meaning he should cleanse himself so as not to commit transgressions. Thus he should habituate himself all year.

**(13) And with this the maiden came, etc.** — **The explanation is:** If he conducts himself thus all year—half in repentance and casting away desire and doing good deeds, and half in positive and negative commandments—afterward he can come to the King on Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur to request, and they do not mention his first iniquities to him at all.
**Whatever she would say would be given to her, etc.** — **The explanation is:** That the Holy One, Blessed be He, always fulfills the will of the righteous and does their desire, etc., and protects them. But not in this world; for Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa was a great righteous man and pious, yet he had nothing to eat except in the World to Come. Also, immediately after death, when one goes from this world to the World to Come, many evil accusers stand on the way. One also needs to pass through Gehinnom, even a perfectly righteous person. But if he did as mentioned above, they protect him so that he is not harmed, Heaven forbid. This is "whatever she would say, etc., to come with her from the house of the women"—which is this world—"to the house of the King"—which is the World to Come.

## Continuation of Peshat

**(14) Guardian of the concubines** — **The explanation is:** For she who had been intimate with the king was forbidden to all others, as it says Abishag the Shunammite was forbidden to Adonijah but permitted to Solomon. Therefore he did not send her to her home, but she remained in the hand of Shaashgaz and became a concubine.
**She would not come again to the king, etc.** — For the virgins each had a set time of twelve months, but the concubines had no set time. "Unless he desired her and she was called by name"—for each one's name was written before the king.

**(15) And when the turn of Esther came, etc.** — As they said in the Midrash: "'Your righteousness is like the mighty mountains, Your judgments are a great deep' (Psalms 36:7)—when the Holy One, Blessed be He, performs a miracle through a righteous person, when He mentions the righteous person He mentions his lineage; but when He performs judgment on a wicked person, He does not mention his lineage." Therefore it is written here "the daughter of Abihail the uncle of Mordechai, etc."
**She requested nothing** — All this the verse shows us: the greatness of her compulsion, for every moment she was in distress over this.
**And Esther was [obtaining favor]** — **The explanation is:** Even though she was constantly distressed and became greenish (*yerakrokas*) because of this, nevertheless "Esther obtained favor in the eyes of all who saw her." This is what they said in the Gemara (13a): "Esther was greenish." For at first glance it is puzzling, as it is written "and the maiden was of beautiful form and beautiful appearance." But according to what I wrote, it fits well: in truth she was of beautiful form, but because of the greatness of her distress she became greenish; nevertheless, the Holy One, Blessed be He, gave her favor because a "thread of kindness" was drawn over her.

**(16) And Esther was taken** — By others, for even here she was under great compulsion.
**It is the month of Tevet** — In this, the Holy One, Blessed be He, showed that He is the Cause of all causes, and He brought it about that it should be at the time when the nights are long and [the king] would increase in intimacy; through this, the two chamberlains of the king became angry, as they said in the Gemara (13b).
**In the seventh year of his reign** — This shows us that Esther hid herself for three years, as stated in the Jerusalem Talmud (Yalkut 1053). For in the third year was the feast and then Vashti was killed, and she did not come until the seventh year of his reign. Perforce, they took her in the sixth year, and for twelve months she was being adorned; therefore she came in the seventh year.

**(17) And the king loved Esther, etc.** — **The explanation is:** Regarding a virgin, the concept of "a beautiful and graceful bride" applies, as mentioned above; therefore it is appropriate to say "favor and kindness (*chen v'chesed*) more than all the virgins." But regarding the women who were already there previously, it only writes "love," for presumably they were beauties whom he had taken while Vashti was still alive; presumably he chose the beautiful ones for himself.
**And he placed the royal crown on her head** — **The explanation is:** He immediately placed the crown on her head and did not wait to see if he might find one more beautiful than her, for he said in his heart that there is none more beautiful than this one in the world.
**And he made her queen in the place of Vashti** — **The explanation is:** She was in his eyes like Vashti, even though she was silent and he did not know her family, and she even said she did not know; nevertheless, she was in his eyes like Vashti, who was a daughter of kings.

**(18) A great feast** — **The explanation is:** An important feast, for the feast became very great in his eyes, like when people say of a person that he is "great"—he is not necessarily great in quantity but in quality. So this feast was in his eyes more important than the first feast, even though it was not as large in quantity, for the first was one hundred and eighty days.
**For all his princes and servants, etc.** — Because she was very important in his eyes, he called it by Esther's name; therefore it says "Esther's feast."
**And he made a release for the provinces** — Because he did not know from which family she came, he made a release for the provinces [hoping she would reveal it]. This is what is written later, that when he knew from which family she was, "King Ahasuerus placed a tax, etc." (10:1), as explained (Gemara 13a).
**And he gave gifts** — **The explanation is:** Not only did he make a release, but he gave great and important gifts according to the king's bounty.

**(19) And when the virgins were gathered a second time, etc.** — **The explanation is:** Afterward they gathered all the remaining virgins to send them to their homes. They wrote this to show the greatness of Esther's righteousness, as will be explained.
**And Mordechai sat in the king's gate** — **The explanation is:** To everyone he gave a release and gifts. And for Mordechai, who was a Jew, he nevertheless needed to do something great for him; for this, the king made him sit in the king's gate, meaning among the king's princes.

**(20) Esther did not tell** — **The explanation is:** Even though she saw she was great in the king's eyes and he was making releases for all the provinces for her sake, and she saw they were sending away all the virgins and he no longer desired them, and they also seated Mordechai among the king's princes—and certainly if he knew what he was to her [he would do more]—nevertheless, "Esther did not tell." Rashi explains: because Mordechai sat in the king's gate, he commanded her and urged her not to tell.
**And the word (*ma'amar*) of Mordechai** — **The explanation is:** Even a light word.

**(21) Of the guardians of the threshold** — **The explanation is:** For the guardians of the threshold are three, as stated in the Gemara (23a); therefore it says "of the guardians of the threshold." In the Gemara it says they said: "Is my watch and your watch not [the same]?" Now, this is not understood. It appears that because they were great princes standing on guard, and they were three, they divided the three parts of the king's night. In that plot, two of them were involved, and they said: "Behold, we are two, and one must go to bring the poison and prepare it, and this requires much time, yet I must stand on guard." The second replied: "I will guard my watch and your watch." This is "and it was found"—they said they found him in a watch that was not his, for the second had gone to prepare the poison.

**(22) In the name of Mordechai** — She did not take the greatness for herself, but told the king in his name, because she understood that a miracle would arise from this.

**(23) Before the king** — To show the causes of the Holy One, Blessed be He: for usually everything in the chronicles is written not in the presence of the king, but this was written "before the king," as I will explain with Heaven's help.
